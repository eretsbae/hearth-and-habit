Most advice about lowering your electric bill starts with spending money: a smart thermostat, new windows, a heat pump. All fine ideas — eventually. But before you buy anything, it's worth capturing the savings that cost literally nothing, because for most households the no-cost habits below can trim a meaningful slice off the bill, often in the range of 5–15% depending on your climate and starting habits.

They're ranked roughly by impact, so start at the top.

## 1. Move your thermostat a few degrees

Heating and cooling typically account for the largest share of a home's energy use — often around 40–50% — which makes the thermostat the highest-leverage dial in your house.

The habit: **in summer, set it a few degrees warmer when you're asleep or away; in winter, a few degrees cooler.** A common rule of thumb from energy utilities is that a 7–10°F setback for 8 hours a day can save roughly 10% a year on heating and cooling. You don't need a smart thermostat to do this — a $0 habit or your existing programmable thermostat's schedule button does the job.

Comfort tip: change it gradually. One degree per week barely registers, and ceiling fans (which you already own) let you feel comfortable a few degrees warmer in summer.

## 2. Wash clothes in cold water

Roughly 85–90% of a washing machine's energy goes to heating water. Modern detergents are formulated to work in cold water, and for everyday laundry the cleaning difference is negligible. Save hot washes for the rare genuinely germy load (sickness, cloth diapers). This one is a single settings change that pays every load, forever.

## 3. Stop the dryer from double-drying

The dryer is one of the hungriest appliances in the house. Three free upgrades to how you already use it:

- **Clean the lint screen every single load** — restricted airflow means longer cycles
- **Don't overstuff** — clothes need tumbling room to dry efficiently
- **Use the moisture sensor setting** instead of timed dry, so the machine stops when clothes are dry rather than when the timer says so

And in warm months, the clothesline or a drying rack is the ultimate zero-cost dryer.

## 4. Hunt down phantom loads

Electronics sip power around the clock even when "off" — TVs, game consoles, cable boxes, chargers, coffee makers with clocks. Individually small, collectively real: studies of standby power routinely attribute roughly 5–10% of residential electricity use to devices that are ostensibly turned off.

The zero-cost version: **unplug what you rarely use** (the guest-room TV, the second printer) and group frequently used clusters onto power strips you already own, flipping one switch at night. Game consoles deserve special attention — check their settings for an energy-saver or "shut down instead of rest" mode.

## 5. Run full loads, and run them at night if you have time-of-use rates

Dishwashers and washing machines use nearly the same energy whether half full or full — so full loads mean fewer cycles. Then check your utility bill (or website) for **time-of-use pricing**. Many North American utilities now charge less at night and on weekends. If yours does, shifting the dishwasher and laundry to off-peak hours saves money with zero change to how much electricity you use. Most dishwashers even have a delay-start button for exactly this.

## 6. Use the dishwasher's air-dry setting

Heated dry is a surprisingly large chunk of a dishwasher cycle's energy. Switch to air-dry (or just crack the door open after the wash ends). Dishes dry, electrons stay home.

## 7. Let the sun work for you — in both directions

In summer, close blinds and curtains on the sunny side of the house during the day; solar gain through windows makes your AC work noticeably harder. In winter, do the reverse — open them wide on sunny days, close them at dusk to hold the warmth in. It's free passive climate control, and in sunny climates the difference is bigger than most people expect.

## 8. Cook without heating the whole kitchen

The oven is the least efficient way to heat food, especially in summer when its heat becomes your AC's problem. The microwave, toaster oven, slow cooker, or stovetop typically use a fraction of the energy for small and medium jobs. Also skip most preheating waits and resist opening the oven door to peek — each peek can drop the oven temperature by around 25°F.

## 9. Turn things off — but know what matters

Lights are the classic advice, and yes, turn them off when leaving a room (with LEDs the savings are modest, but it's free). The bigger "off" wins are the ones people forget:

- **Bathroom and kitchen exhaust fans** left running for hours — in summer and winter they're actively pumping your conditioned air outdoors
- **A second fridge or freezer in the garage** that's keeping six items cold — consolidate and unplug it; an old spare fridge is frequently among the largest single loads in the house
- **Ceiling fans in empty rooms** — fans cool people, not rooms

## How to tell it's working

Don't judge by the bill total alone — rates and seasons move it around. Compare the **kWh (kilowatt-hours)** line on your bill against the same month last year; most utilities print this comparison right on the statement. Pick two or three habits, run them for a full billing cycle, and check the kWh delta.

## Frequently asked questions

**How much can these habits realistically save?**
It depends heavily on climate, home size, and where your habits start. A household that adopts the thermostat setback, cold-water washing, and phantom-load control typically sees savings in the mid-to-high single digits percentage-wise — often $10–$30 a month in an average North American home. Not life-changing, but it's recurring money for zero dollars invested.

**Isn't it cheaper to keep the house at one temperature than to reheat it?**
This is the most persistent myth in home energy. For conventional systems, the answer is no — the energy saved while the house sits at the setback temperature outweighs the recovery cost. (One notable exception: heat pumps with electric resistance backup can be an exception in deep cold, where a large morning recovery kicks in the expensive backup strips — modest setbacks are safer there.)

**What should I buy first, once I've done the free stuff?**
When you're ready to spend: LED bulbs for any remaining incandescents, weatherstripping for drafty doors, and only then bigger-ticket items. We cover each of those in their own guides.
