Most home maintenance disasters don't start as disasters. They start as a five-dollar part or a ten-minute task that quietly got skipped for two or three years running. The furnace filter gets remembered because it's loud and obvious. The stuff that gets forgotten is usually hidden — behind an appliance, up in an attic, or outside where nobody looks unless something's already leaking.

This checklist walks through the ten tasks that consistently slip through the cracks, organized by when to actually do them. None of these require special tools you don't already own, and most take under 30 minutes. Where a job is genuinely risky or requires licensing, that's flagged too.

## Step 1: Flush the water heater (fall or spring, once a year)

Sediment settles at the bottom of a water heater tank over time, especially in areas with hard water. This buildup makes the unit work harder to heat water and can shorten its lifespan by several years.

- Turn off the power (electric) or set the gas valve to "pilot."
- Connect a garden hose to the drain valve near the bottom and run it to a floor drain or outside.
- Close the cold water supply, open the drain valve, and let it run until the water comes out clear.
- Turn the cold water supply back on briefly to flush out remaining sediment, then close the drain valve and restore power.

**Key takeaway: a tank water heater typically lasts 8–12 years, and skipping this one task is one of the most common reasons people replace theirs early.**

## Step 2: Clean the dryer vent duct, not just the lint trap (twice a year)

Everyone empties the lint trap after every load — that's not the problem. The problem is the duct running from the dryer to the outside wall, which collects lint the trap can't catch. A clogged vent is one of the leading causes of house fires tied to home appliances, and it also makes your dryer run hotter and longer.

Disconnect the duct from the back of the dryer, vacuum out both ends with a hose attachment, and check the exterior vent flap to make sure it opens freely when the dryer runs. If your duct is longer than about 25 feet or has several bends, consider a dryer vent brush kit or a professional cleaning — lint packs into elbows in ways a vacuum hose can't reach.

## Step 3: Test GFCI outlets in kitchens, bathrooms, and garages (twice a year)

GFCI outlets (the ones with "test" and "reset" buttons, designed to cut power instantly if they detect a shock hazard) can fail silently. The outlet keeps working normally, but the safety mechanism itself stops functioning.

Press "test" — the outlet should cut power immediately, and anything plugged in should turn off. Press "reset" to restore it. If test doesn't cut the power, or reset doesn't restore it, the outlet needs replacing. This is a quick DIY swap for anyone comfortable turning off a breaker first, or a cheap call for an electrician.

## Step 4: Inspect and clean gutters and downspouts (spring and fall)

This one isn't forgotten so much as put off — nobody loves getting on a ladder. But clogged gutters send water straight down your foundation walls instead of away from the house, which is a slow, expensive way to end up with a wet basement.

- Clear leaves and debris by hand or with a gutter scoop.
- Flush with a hose and watch where the water goes — it should flow freely to the downspout and away from the house.
- Check that downspout extensions direct water at least 4–6 feet from the foundation.
- Look at the fascia board behind the gutter for soft or discolored wood, a sign water has been sitting.

If your home has tall trees overhanging the roof or you're not comfortable on a ladder above one story, this is a reasonable one to hire out once or twice a year.

## Step 5: Check the attic for signs of moisture or pests (fall, before it gets cold)

![Illustration](/images/forgotten-home-maintenance-tasks-seasonal-checklist-inline.svg)

Most people never go up there unless they're storing holiday decorations. A quick look with a flashlight can catch problems while they're still cheap to fix.

Look for: water stains on the underside of the roof deck, daylight coming through anywhere it shouldn't, insulation that's flattened or displaced (a sign of pests or past water damage), and any droppings or nesting material near vents or eaves. Also confirm that soffit and roof vents aren't blocked by insulation — proper attic airflow prevents ice dams in winter and helps the roof last longer.

## Step 6: Reverse ceiling fan direction for the season

This one is genuinely free and takes about ten seconds per fan, which is probably why it gets overlooked — it feels too simple to matter. In winter, fans should run clockwise (viewed from below) at low speed, which pushes warm air pooled at the ceiling back down. In summer, counterclockwise creates a cooling breeze effect.

Most fans have a small switch on the motor housing; newer remote-controlled models have a button or app setting. It won't transform your energy bill on its own, but paired with the no-cost habits in reducing overall energy use, it's a small consistent win.

## Step 7: Check exterior caulking and weatherstripping (fall)

Gaps around windows, doors, and where pipes or wiring enter the house are easy to forget because they fail slowly. A quarter-inch gap doesn't look dramatic, but over a winter it adds up to real heat loss.

Run a hand around window and door frames feeling for drafts on a windy day, or hold a lit candle or incense stick near the edges — flickering means air movement. Re-caulk any cracked or missing sections with exterior-grade silicone caulk, and replace worn weatherstripping on doors. This is inexpensive (typically $10–20 per tube or roll) and one of the better returns on effort for winter comfort.

## Step 8: Test smoke and carbon monoxide detectors — and check the actual age (twice a year)

Pressing the test button confirms the battery works, but it doesn't confirm the sensor itself is still reliable. Smoke detectors typically need full replacement every 8–10 years, and CO detectors roughly every 5–7 years, regardless of how new the batteries are. Check the manufacture date printed on the back of the unit.

While testing, replace batteries in units that use them (even if they're not dead yet — a rule of thumb many people use is to swap batteries when clocks change in spring and fall). If a detector is silent when tested or past its printed expiration, replace the whole unit — this isn't one to patch.

## Step 9: Inspect the roof from the ground with binoculars (spring and fall)

Nobody wants to climb onto a roof, and in most cases you don't need to. A pair of binoculars from the yard can reveal a surprising amount.

Look for shingles that are curling, cracked, or missing granules (they'll look bald or shiny in patches), rusty or lifted flashing around chimneys and vents, and any sagging along the roofline. Catching a small area of damage early is the difference between a $300 repair and a full re-roof after water gets under the deck. If you see anything concerning, this is a call-a-pro situation — roof inspections and repairs carry real fall risk and are worth the cost of a licensed roofer.

## Step 10: Clean the refrigerator coils (once a year)

The coils on the back or underneath the fridge release heat as part of the cooling cycle. When they're coated in dust and pet hair, the compressor has to work harder and the fridge's efficiency drops — sometimes enough to notice on the electric bill.

Unplug the fridge, pull it out from the wall, and vacuum the coils with a brush attachment or a dedicated coil-cleaning brush. If your coils are underneath rather than in back, you may need to remove a front grille panel, which usually just pops or unscrews off. Ten minutes, once a year, is genuinely all it takes.

## Putting it together: a simple seasonal rhythm

Trying to remember all ten tasks at once is how they get forgotten in the first place. A more realistic approach is to pair a couple with seasonal transitions you're already aware of:

- **Spring:** gutters, roof check, water heater flush
- **Summer:** fan direction switch, GFCI test
- **Fall:** attic check, caulking, gutters again, roof check again, detector batteries
- **Any time, once a year:** dryer vent deep clean, fridge coils

None of these need to happen on a specific weekend — the goal is just to make sure each one happens at least once in its season, not that it happens perfectly on schedule.

## FAQ

**Do I really need to do the roof and gutter checks twice a year?**
It's worth it in most climates, since spring reveals winter damage (ice, snow load, freeze-thaw cracking) and fall prep matters before the next winter hits. In milder climates with fewer trees, once a year may be enough — use judgment based on your actual conditions.

**What's the single task on this list most likely to save real money?**
The water heater flush and dryer vent cleaning tend to have the clearest financial payoff, since both affect appliance lifespan and, in the dryer's case, fire risk. Neither costs anything beyond your time.

**Which of these should I never attempt myself?**
Actual roof repair (not just the binocular inspection) and any electrical work beyond testing and swapping a GFCI outlet with the breaker off. Both carry real safety risk, and licensed pros carry insurance for a reason.

**I'm a renter — should I still be doing this?**
Several of these — GFCI testing, detector checks, fridge coils, fan direction — are worth doing regardless of who owns the place, since they affect your safety and comfort. Gutters, roofing, and water heater maintenance are typically the landlord's responsibility; it's reasonable to flag issues rather than fix them yourself.
