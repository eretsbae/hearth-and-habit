Turn off every light and appliance in your house, then walk through it at night. The soft glow you still see — a cable box, a phone charger, a microwave clock — is phantom power in action. It's electricity being drawn by devices that are technically "off" but never fully stop pulling from the outlet.

Individually, none of these devices cost much. But most homes have 20 to 40 of them running around the clock, 365 days a year. That adds up to a real chunk of the average electric bill — commonly estimated at somewhere between 5% and 10% of total household electricity use, depending on how much gear you own and how old it is. This is a different problem than the habits covered in cutting your electric bill without buying anything, because the waste here happens whether you're home or not, whether you're paying attention or not.

Think of this as a walkthrough checklist. Go room by room, check off what applies to your house, and fix the worst offenders first.

## Why Phantom Power Happens in the First Place

Before the checklist, it helps to know what you're actually hunting for.

**Anything with a remote control, a clock, a light, or a "quick start" feature is drawing power even when off.** These devices stay in a low-power standby mode so they can respond instantly — a TV listening for the remote, a microwave keeping its clock accurate, a game console ready to update overnight. Manufacturers call this "standby load" or "vampire power," and it's built into the product by design, not a defect.

The other big source is anything with an external power adapter — those black brick-shaped transformers on phone chargers, laptop chargers, and routers. Many of these draw a small amount of power just by being plugged into the wall, even with nothing connected on the other end.

**Key takeaway:** if a device turns on instantly with a remote or app, or if it has a brick-style plug, assume it's drawing power 24/7 unless you unplug it.

## The Living Room and Entertainment Center

- **Cable or satellite box.** Often the single worst offender in the house. DVR boxes in particular run constantly to record scheduled shows, so they draw nearly as much power "off" as "on." This is one device where a smart plug on a timer isn't ideal, since it needs to stay live to function — but a basic box that's not a DVR is safer to power down at night.
- **Game consoles.** Left in "instant-on" or rest mode for quick startup and automatic updates, these can draw a surprising amount for something that looks fully off. Switching the setting to a genuine power-off mode (check the console's power settings menu) cuts this dramatically.
- **TVs and soundbars.** The standby light you see is real draw, though usually modest per device. The bigger cost is often the streaming stick or smart TV apps checking for updates in the background.
- **Streaming sticks and smart speakers.** Small individually, but people often own three or four of them, and they never really sleep.

A power strip with a physical switch is the simplest fix here: plug the TV, soundbar, console, and streaming stick into one strip, and flip it off when you're done for the night. The DVR box should stay on its own separate outlet.

## The Kitchen

- **Microwave.** The clock and keypad draw power continuously. There's no practical fix for this one short of unplugging it between uses, which most people won't do — it's a small, accepted cost.
- **Coffee maker with a clock or programmable timer.** If you don't use the programmable start feature, unplugging it after each use (or plugging it into a switched outlet) removes a small but constant draw.
- **Toaster oven, air fryer, or instant pot with digital displays.** Anything with a lit-up screen or touch panel is drawing power at all hours, not just while cooking.
- **Dishwasher and range with electronic controls.** These typically draw very little in standby, but they're worth knowing about since they're wired directly into circuits rather than plugged into an outlet, so there's no easy switch — this one you generally leave alone.

**A good rule of thumb:** if it has a digital clock or display and you don't rely on that clock, it's a candidate for unplugging.

## The Home Office

- **Desktop computer and monitor.** A computer left in "sleep" still draws meaningful power, especially older desktops. A full shutdown draws far less than sleep mode, and modern computers start up fast enough that the convenience trade-off is small.
- **Router and modem.** These need to stay on for internet access, so this isn't a phantom-power target to eliminate — but it's worth noting as one of the few devices in the house that legitimately should run continuously.
- **Printer.** Many printers sit in standby for weeks between uses. A switched power strip is an easy fix since printers rarely need instant-on availability.
- **Phone and laptop chargers left plugged in with nothing attached.** The brick itself draws a small trickle of power just sitting in the wall. It's a minor amount per charger, but most households have five or more scattered around, and it's genuinely free to fix — just unplug the ones not actively charging something.

## The Bedroom and Bathroom

- **Phone chargers on the nightstand.** Same issue as the office — unplug when not charging, especially if you don't use the phone as an alarm overnight.
- **Box fans, humidifiers, and space heaters with digital controls.** Anything seasonal that sits plugged in year-round after its season has passed is pure waste. This overlaps with seasonal maintenance habits worth building into a seasonal changeover routine — unplug it when you put it away, not just when you turn it off.
- **Electric toothbrush chargers and hair tools with standby lights.** Small draws, but the charger base often stays plugged in permanently even though it's only used for two minutes a day.

## The Laundry Room and Garage

- **Washer and dryer with electronic displays.** Similar to kitchen appliances — a small standby draw that's part of normal operation and not worth fighting.
- **Garage door opener.** Needs to stay powered to work, so leave it be; this is a case where "always on" is the correct choice, not an oversight.
- **Chargers for power tools, e-bikes, or lawn equipment.** These often pull noticeable power even after the battery is full, because the charger keeps "topping off." Unplugging once charging is done, rather than leaving it connected indefinitely, both saves power and is generally better for battery lifespan.

## How to Prioritize: Start With the Big Ones

Not every phantom load is worth chasing. A phone charger with nothing plugged into it wastes a trivial amount. The bigger returns come from:

1. **DVR and cable boxes** — often the largest single standby draw in a house.
2. **Game consoles left in instant-on mode** — an easy settings change with a real impact.
3. **Anything with a large power brick that's rarely used** — printers, unused monitors, extra routers.
4. **Seasonal devices left plugged in year-round** — space heaters, fans, holiday lighting transformers.

A smart plug or a switched power strip on these four categories will capture most of the realistic savings without requiring you to unplug your microwave clock every night.

## FAQ

**Will unplugging everything actually show up on my electric bill?**
You likely won't see a dramatic drop from one device, but tackling the entertainment center and any DVR-free boxes as a group is often noticeable over a full year, especially in a house with a lot of electronics.

**Are smart plugs worth buying for this?**
For devices you want to fully cut off on a schedule — printers, TVs, game consoles — yes, they're a reasonable low-cost tool. Avoid using them on anything that needs to stay live, like routers, DVR boxes, or the fridge.

**Is phantom power worse with older electronics?**
Generally yes. Newer devices are typically built to stricter standby-efficiency standards, so replacing a very old cable box, printer, or game console can reduce standby draw even before you unplug anything.

**Does turning things off at the wall damage them?**
No, for the vast majority of consumer electronics. The main exception is anything mid-update or a DVR mid-recording — check before you cut power to those.

The point isn't to unplug your whole house every night. It's to notice which devices are drawing power for no reason and give the biggest ones an easy off-switch — then let the smaller stuff go.
